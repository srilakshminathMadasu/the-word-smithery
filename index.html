CAESAR SLAIN ON THE IDES OF MARCH

The Dictator Perpetuo falls to a band of Senators; the Eternal City trembles as the fate of the Republic hangs by a thread upon this most dreadful day

Conspirators Strike at the Heart of Rome

By Gaius Scribonius · Senate Correspondent

Julius Caesar, Dictator Perpetuo of Rome, was this morning assassinated within the Theatre of Pompey, falling before a assembly of some twenty-three Senators armed with daggers concealed beneath their togas. The attack came as Caesar took his seat, at which moment the conspirators descended upon him.

Sources present at the scene report that Casca struck the first blow, followed swiftly by the others. Caesar is said to have defended himself briefly before recognizing among his attackers the face of Marcus Junius Brutus, at which moment he ceased to struggle and drew his toga about his face to meet his end with dignity.

"Et tu, Bruté? — Then fall, Caesar."
— Last words attributed to Gaius Julius Caesar

The body of the Dictator was discovered to bear three-and-twenty wounds, though physicians summoned to the scene report that but a single wound to the chest proved fatal. The Senate hall fell into immediate panic, with many senators and citizens fleeing into the streets of Rome.

Brutus Addresses the Citizens: "Rome is Free"

By Publius Velius · Forum Reporter

In the immediate hours following the assassination, Marcus Junius Brutus descended to the Forum Romanum and addressed the gathered populace, defending the actions of those he called liberators. He declared the deed was done not out of malice toward Caesar the man, but out of love for Rome and fear of tyranny.

The crowd, stunned and uncertain, appeared briefly swayed by Brutus's eloquent reasoning. He spoke of honour, of the Republic, and of the freedoms imperilled by one man's unchecked ambition, drawing comparisons to the expulsion of King Tarquinius Superbus of centuries past.

However, the mood shifted dramatically when Mark Antony, Caesar's loyal lieutenant and consul, took the rostrum and delivered a funeral oration over Caesar's body that moved the Roman mob to tears — and then to fury.

Antony's Oration Turns the Tide of Public Opinion

By Flavia Maxima · Forum Correspondent

Mark Antony, granted the right to speak at Caesar's funeral by a perhaps overly trusting Brutus, delivered an oration that those present shall not soon forget. Beginning with the words now repeated throughout Rome — "Friends, Romans, countrymen" — he spoke with calculated precision.

Antony repeatedly styled Brutus an "honourable man" with an irony so pointed as to provoke the assembled citizens to rage. He displayed Caesar's bloodied, wound-covered toga to the crowd, and is said to have read aloud a will bequeathing Caesar's private gardens to the people and seventy-five drachmas to every Roman citizen.

"The evil that men do lives after them; the good is oft interred with their bones."
— Mark Antony, at Caesar's funeral

By the time Antony had finished speaking, the Roman mob had turned entirely against the conspirators. Riots erupted across the city; Brutus, Cassius, and their faction have been forced to flee Rome entirely. Civil war, this correspondent fears, is all but inevitable.

DRAMATIS PERSONAE — THE PRINCIPAL ACTORS IN THIS TRAGEDY
C

Julius Caesar

Dictator of Rome

The most powerful man in the Roman world. Ambitious, magnanimous, and beloved by the common people, he was feared by many in the Senate as a threat to the Republic.

B

Marcus Brutus

Conspirator · Senator

A man of deep honour and genuine republican ideals, Brutus was convinced by Cassius that Caesar's death was necessary to preserve Roman freedom — a decision he may yet regret.

A

Mark Antony

Consul · Caesar's Ally

Loyal to Caesar and politically astute, Antony turned Caesar's funeral into a masterwork of rhetorical manipulation, unleashing the fury of Rome upon the conspirators.

C

Gaius Cassius

Conspirator · Senator

The architect of the conspiracy. A lean and hungry man, Cassius harboured personal grudges against Caesar and the vision necessary to recruit Brutus as the plot's moral cornerstone.

ANALYSIS

The Ghost of Caesar and the Fall of the Conspirators

By Lucius Veranius · Senior Political Correspondent

In the days and weeks following the assassination, the faction of Brutus and Cassius found themselves not the liberators of Rome they imagined, but hunted men. Mark Antony, joined by the young Octavian — Caesar's adopted heir — and the general Lepidus, formed the Second Triumvirate and moved against the conspirators with overwhelming force.

The two armies met at the plains of Philippi in Macedonia. There, in two decisive engagements, the republican forces were broken. Cassius, believing mistakenly that Brutus too had been defeated, fell upon his own sword. Brutus, after suffering a final defeat, followed suit — reportedly declaring that Caesar's ghost had at last been avenged.

Thus did the conspirators' enterprise end not in the restoration of the Republic they so dearly wished to preserve, but in its further destruction. The irony shall be remarked upon by historians for centuries to come: the men who killed Caesar to prevent a monarchy gave Rome an emperor.

⚡ Omens & Portents ⚡

Citizens report with great alarm that in the days preceding the Ides, numerous terrible omens were observed throughout Rome. A soothsayer is said to have warned Caesar directly: "Beware the Ides of March."

Caesar's wife, Calpurnia, besought him not to attend the Senate, having been visited in the night by dreadful dreams of his statue spouting blood. Lions were reportedly seen whelping in the streets. Graves yielded up their dead. And above the Capitol, fiery warriors fought in the clouds.

Caesar dismissed all such warnings, declaring he was not afraid of death. He was, say those close to him, perhaps the only man in Rome who was not.

Portia's Sacrifice

Brutus's devoted wife Portia, having been privy to the conspiracy and unable to bear her husband's disgrace and flight, is reported to have taken her own life by swallowing hot coals. Rome mourns her.

The Young Octavian

Caesar's great-nephew and adopted son Gaius Octavius, known henceforth as Octavian, returns to Rome to claim his inheritance. Observers note an uncommon shrewdness in the young man that belies his eighteen years.

TIMELINE

The Play: Act by Act

ACT I

Tribunes Flavius and Marullus chastise crowds celebrating Caesar's triumph. A soothsayer warns Caesar of the Ides of March. Cassius begins seducing Brutus toward conspiracy.

ACT II

Brutus, tormented, resolves to join the conspiracy. Portia pleads with him. Caesar ignores Calpurnia's dreams and Artemidorus's letter of warning, and proceeds to the Capitol.

ACT III

The assassination. Antony's funeral oration. The mob erupts and Rome descends into riot. The conspirators flee the city.

ACT IV

The Triumvirate consolidates power. Brutus and Cassius quarrel and reconcile. Brutus is visited by the Ghost of Caesar, who promises to meet him at Philippi.

ACT V

The Battle of Philippi. Cassius and Brutus both die by their own hands. Antony eulogises Brutus as "the noblest Roman of them all."

COMMENTARY

Themes of the Age

Shakespeare's tragedy, performed at the Globe Theatre, compels its audience to grapple with questions as urgent today as in Caesar's Rome. Chief among these is the tension between personal honour and political expediency — a tension embodied in Brutus, who kills a friend out of principle and destroys himself in the process.

The play also asks: what makes a tyrant? Caesar is ambitious, yes — but is ambition alone sufficient cause for murder? Cassius's motivations are revealed as far less noble than Brutus's, suggesting the conspiracy is rotten at its core from the outset.

Power corrupts not only its holders but those who would remove it. Antony, ostensibly avenging Caesar for noble reasons, proves himself every bit as manipulative as Cassius. The Republic, it seems, was doomed regardless of the Ides of March.

Most haunting of all is the play's treatment of fate. Soothsayers, omens, and ghosts suggest that Caesar's end — and the Republic's — was written long before any conspirator raised a blade.
